Chapter 2: Engineering Materials

This chapter introduces the fundamental concepts of engineering materials, focusing on their classification, structure, and particularly their mechanical properties. Understanding these properties is crucial for selecting appropriate materials for manufacturing specific products and for predicting their behavior during processing and in service. The chapter also covers common engineering materials and the factors involved in material selection, along with solved examples illustrating the calculation of mechanical properties.

2.1 Introduction

  • Materials are the foundation of all technological advances. Knowledge of their properties and behavioral characteristics is crucial.
  • Products like automobiles consist of many parts made from different materials, each chosen for its specific function and properties (e.g., steel for car body due to strength and formability, plastics for dashboards due to light weight and mouldability).
  • Engineers consider material properties for the application (e.g., hardness for mechanical use, conductivity for electrical use), behavior during manufacturing, and environmental impact.
  • Major classes of materials:
    1. Metals: (e.g., iron, copper, aluminum).
    2. Ceramics: (e.g., sand, bricks, glass).
    3. Polymers: (e.g., nylon, Teflon, polyethylene).
    4. Composites: (e.g., carbon fibers in epoxy, used in tennis rackets).
    5. Semiconductors: (e.g., silicon, germanium, used in transistors).
  • There's an intimate link between a material's structure (atomic arrangement) and its properties (mechanical, electrical, thermal, etc.).
  • Processing affects structure, which in turn affects properties.
    • Example: Steel slowly cooled from high temperature is soft. If quenched (rapidly cooled), the same steel becomes extremely hard and brittle due to changes in its structure.

2.2 Structure of Materials

Understanding material structure is key to understanding its properties and behavior. This section focuses mainly on metals.

2.2.1 Crystalline structures of metals

  • When metals solidify, atoms arrange themselves into specific, orderly 3-D patterns called crystals.
  • Space lattice: The 3-D geometrical arrangement of atom centers in a crystal.
  • Unit cell: The smallest repeating volume of the space lattice that represents the entire crystal structure (analogous to a brick in a wall). Most metals have cubic or hexagonal unit cells.
  • Three common crystal lattice structures (Figure 2.1):
    1. Face Centred Cubic (FCC) (Fig. 2.1a): Unit cell is a cube with atoms at each corner and one at the center of each of the six faces.
      • Examples: Copper, gold, nickel, aluminum.
      • Features: Good ductility and electrical conductivity.
    2. Body Centred Cubic (BCC) (Fig. 2.1b): Unit cell is a cube with atoms at each corner and one atom at the geometric center of the cube.
      • Examples: Iron, sodium, vanadium, molybdenum.
      • Features: Tend to deform plastically.
    3. Hexagonal Closely Packed (HCP) (Fig. 2.1c): Unit cell is a hexagonal prism with atoms at each corner, one atom in the center of the top and bottom faces, and three atoms in the midplane between the top and bottom faces.
      • Examples: Zinc, cadmium, cobalt, titanium.
      • Features: Good ductility, easily deformed.
  • Polymorphic: A material that can exist in more than one crystal lattice type, often changing with temperature.
  • X-ray diffraction: A technique used to determine crystal structure and atom positions.
  • Non-crystalline or Amorphous materials: Atoms are not arranged in a regular lattice (e.g., thermoset plastics, transparent polymers, rubber, metallic glasses).

2.3 Properties of Materials

  • Material properties include physical, chemical, mechanical, electrical, etc.
  • Physical properties: e.g., plastics for pens (lightweight), copper for wires (good conductor).
  • Chemical properties: e.g., iron corrodes with water.
  • Electrical properties: Related to conductivity. Impurities reduce metal conductivity but can increase semiconductor conductivity (e.g., phosphorus in silicon).
  • Mechanical properties: Describe material behavior under mechanical forces/loads (e.g., strength, hardness, ductility). These are critical for manufacturing process selection. Cast iron, being brittle, cannot be formed easily.

2.4 Mechanical Properties

Properties like elasticity, ductility, hardness, toughness relate to a material's ability to resist deformation or deform under forces.

Mechanical properties are defined to be independent of specimen size/geometry for comparison.

2.4.1 Types of stresses (Figure 2.2)

  • Tensile load (Fig. 2.2a): Equal and opposite forces pulling a body, causing elongation. The internal resisting stress is tensile stress.
  • Compressive load (Fig. 2.2b): Equal and opposite forces pushing into a body, causing shortening. The internal resisting stress is compressive stress.
  • Shear load (Fig. 2.2c): Opposite forces acting radially across a section. The internal resisting stress is shear stress (or transverse stress).
  • Stress (s or $\sigma$): Force per unit area. SI unit: N/m$^2$ (Pascal, Pa). Stress ($s$) = $F / A_0$ (Eq. 2.1) where F is force, $A_0$ is original cross-sectional area.
  • Strain (e or $\epsilon$): Measure of deformation; ratio of change in dimension to original dimension (dimensionless, e.g., mm/mm). Strain ($e$) = $\Delta L / L_0$ (Eq. 2.2) where $\Delta L$ is change in length, $L_0$ is original length.
  • Deformation under tensile load (Figure 2.3): A cylinder of original diameter $D_0$ and length $L_0$, when pulled by force F, elongates to $L_1$ and its diameter contracts to $D_1$.
    • Linear strain (axial strain): Strain in the direction of applied load.
    • Lateral strain (perpendicular strain): Strain at right angles to the applied load.
    • Poisson's ratio: Ratio of lateral strain to linear strain (discussed later).

2.4.2 Stress-strain relationship (Figure 2.4, Figure 2.5)

  • A cylindrical rod (ductile material) subjected to increasing tensile load elongates until it breaks (Fig. 2.4).
  • Plotting stress vs. strain gives the stress-strain curve (Fig. 2.5).
  • (Fig. 2.5a: Stress-strain curve for brass; Fig. 2.5b: Stress-strain curve for mild steel)
  • Elastic Region (Origin to P or Q): Material deforms but returns to original shape when stress is removed.
    • Proportional Limit (P): Point up to which stress is directly proportional to strain (linear relationship).
    • Elastic Limit (Q): Maximum stress a material can withstand without permanent deformation. Often close to P.
    • Young's Modulus (E) / Modulus of Elasticity: Ratio of stress to strain in the linear elastic region. It is the slope of the linear portion. $E = \text{Stress / Strain} = \sigma / \epsilon$ (Eq. 2.3). $E = (F/A_0) / (\Delta L/L_0) = FL_0 / (A_0\Delta L)$ (Eq. 2.4). It measures stiffness: resistance to elastic deformation. A stiff material has a high E.
    • Elongation $\Delta L = FL_0 / (A_0E)$ (Eq. 2.5).
  • Plastic Region (Right of P or Q): Material undergoes permanent deformation.
    • Yield Point (Yield Strength, Y on Fig. 2.5a): Stress at which material begins to deform plastically without a significant increase in stress (or with a specified permanent strain, e.g., 0.2% offset method for materials like brass, as shown by dotted line in Fig. 2.5a). Mild steel (Fig. 2.5b) shows a distinct yield point (C).
    • Ultimate Tensile Strength (UTS) (T on Fig. 2.5a & b): Maximum stress the material can withstand before necking (localized reduction in cross-section) begins in ductile materials.
    • Breaking Strength / Rupture Strength (R on Fig. 2.5a): Stress at which the material fractures.
  • Brittle Materials (Figure 2.6): These materials (e.g., cast iron, glass) show little to no plastic deformation. They fracture at or near the ultimate tensile strength, often without yielding.

2.4.3 Toughness

  • Ability of a material to absorb energy (withstand sudden load) without failure.
  • Depends on both strength and ductility.
  • Measured by the total area under the stress-strain curve up to fracture.
  • Important for impact loads (e.g., milling cutters, gears).

2.4.4 Hardness

  • Resistance to mechanical indentation, scratching, or wear.
  • General indication of strength. Diamond is the hardest.
  • Important for tools and machine tool structures. Hard materials are difficult to shape.

2.4.5 Ductility

  • Extent to which a material can sustain plastic deformation under tension before rupture.
  • Indicates ability to be drawn into wires.
  • Example: Gold (28.35g can be drawn into 80 km of thin wire).
  • Important for forming and bending (e.g., automobile body).
  • Materials with little or no ductility are brittle.

2.4.6 Brittleness

  • Material undergoes very little plastic deformation before rupture.
  • Exhibits no yielding or necking. Ruptures suddenly at ultimate strength.
  • Weak and unreliable in tension.
  • Examples: Cast iron, glass, ceramics.

2.4.7 Malleability

  • Ability to be flattened (deformed) into thin sheets under compression without cracking.
  • Different from ductility (tensile quality). Malleability is a compressive quality.
  • Example: Lead is malleable but not very ductile. Silver is both.
  • Examples: Aluminum, lead, copper, tin.

2.4.8 Resilience

  • Capacity of a material to absorb energy elastically (without permanent deformation).
  • Energy is released upon load removal (like a spring).
  • Measured by the triangular area under the elastic portion of the stress-strain curve (Fig. 2.5).
  • Important for shock absorbers, springs.

2.4.9 Stiffness

  • Ability of a material to resist deformation (elastic).
  • A material with a high Young's modulus (E) is stiffer.
  • Example: Steel (E $\approx$ 210 GPa) is three times stiffer than aluminum (E $\approx$ 70 GPa). For the same stress and cross-section, aluminum deforms three times more than steel.

2.4.10 Poisson's ratio ($\nu$) (Figure 2.7)

  • When a bar is subjected to axial tensile load, it elongates axially and contracts transversely (Fig. 2.7a). Under compression, it contracts axially and expands transversely (Fig. 2.7b).
  • Poisson's ratio is the ratio of transverse strain ($\epsilon_t$) to axial strain ($\epsilon_a$) within the proportionality limit. It's a material constant.

    Axial strain $\epsilon_a = \delta/L$

    Transverse strain $\epsilon_t = \delta b/b$ (where b is original transverse dimension, $\delta b$ is change in b)

    Poisson's ratio $v = -\epsilon_t / \epsilon_a$ (Eq. 2.6) (The negative sign indicates that axial elongation causes transverse contraction, and vice versa).

2.5 Modes of Fracture

Understanding how and why materials fail. Determined by crystal structure, purity, properties, temperature, stress state, loading rate, environment.

Four general classes of fracture mechanisms:

  1. 2.5.1 Ductile fracture (Figure 2.8)

    • Occurs after significant plastic deformation, typically above the yield point.
    • Stages (Fig 2.8b-f): Stress concentration, formation of small pores (Fig 2.8c), necking (reduction of cross-section area), pores join to form an internal cavity (Fig 2.8e), rim fails by shear (often at 45° to tensile axis, Fig 2.8f).
    • Requires considerable energy for plastic deformation in the necking region.
    • Important in metalworking (deep drawing, forging).

  2. 2.5.2 Brittle fracture

    • Little or no plastic deformation before fracture. Cracks propagate rapidly.
    • Brittle metals are weak as they offer little resistance to crack propagation. Ductile metals deform rather than allowing cracks to propagate.
    • Can occur at stresses below yield strength, especially under impact/shock loads, often without warning, leading to catastrophic failure.
    • More likely in large components or under shock loading.

  3. 2.5.3 Creep fracture

    • Time-dependent deformation and fracture under constant load, especially at elevated temperatures (can occur below room temp for materials like lead/aluminum, or very high temp for alloy steels).
    • Material continues to deform (elongate) over time, even if load is constant, until usefulness is impaired or fracture occurs.
    • Creep: Plastic/inelastic distortion under long-term loading.
    • Amount depends on elasticity, yield strength, stress, temperature.
    • Critical in design of steam turbines, boilers, jet engine blades (e.g., turbine blades at 1200°C).

  4. 2.5.4 Fatigue fracture

    • Failure due to cyclic or repeated stresses, often at stress levels well below the static yield strength.
    • Occurs suddenly and unexpectedly. Described as "progressive fracture."
    • Mechanism involves initiation of microcracks (due to crystal imperfections or surface flaws) that grow with each stress cycle until the part fails.
    • Important for components under alternating loads (e.g., bending a paper clip back and forth).

2.6 Factor of Safety

Design work assumes material homogeneity, which is not always true. Uncertainties exist in material properties, service conditions, and potential defects (cracks, blowholes).

To avoid failure, design stress is kept well within the yield stress (for ductile materials) or ultimate stress (for brittle materials).

  • Factor of safety (FoS) or safety factor: A number used to achieve this.
    • $FoS = \text{Yield stress / Design stress}$ (for ductile materials) (Eq. 2.7)
    • $FoS = \text{Ultimate stress / Design stress}$ (for brittle materials) (Eq. 2.8)
  • Example: If design criteria stress is 500 N/m$^2$ and yield strength is 1000 N/m$^2$, FoS = 2. This accounts for uncertainties in properties, wear, surface finish, manufacturing inaccuracies.
  • FoS varies with application. Higher for fatigue loads, critical applications (airplane parts, satellites) involving risk to human life.
  • Factors considered when choosing FoS:
    1. Variation/non-homogeneity in material properties.
    2. Uncertainty in analysis methods and manufacturing.
    3. Environmental conditions.
    4. Type of application.

2.7 Common Engineering Materials

Metals are seldom used in pure state; usually as alloys.

2.7.1 Cast iron

  • Ferrous alloy: Iron + 2.1% to 4.5% Carbon + ~3.5% Silicon.
  • Hard, brittle. Free graphite gives self-lubricating properties. Excellent vibration damping.
  • Good compressive strength, weak in tension.
  • Used for machine beds, frames.
  • Types:
    • Gray cast iron: Good wear resistance (e.g., machine tool guideways).
    • Malleable cast iron: More tensile, impact, fatigue strength than gray CI (e.g., agricultural/textile machinery parts).
    • Spheroidal-graphite (SG) cast iron / Ductile Iron: Strength, toughness, ductility similar to steel (e.g., crankshafts, connecting rods).

2.7.2 Steel

  • Most common engineering material. Iron + Carbon alloy (typically < 2.1% C), may contain other elements (Mn, Si, Cr, Cu).
  • Properties can be manipulated by heat treatment.
  • Classification by carbon content (see Table 2.1 for applications):
    1. Low carbon steel / Mild steel (0.05 to 0.3%C): Thin sheets, tubes, wire, structural sections, boilers.
    2. Medium carbon steel (0.3 to 0.7%C): Agricultural implements, axles, hammers, springs, dies.
    3. High carbon steel (0.7 to 1.5%C): Cutting blades, chisels, woodworking tools, metal cutting tools, razor blades, files, drills.

2.7.3 Aluminium

  • Non-ferrous. Excellent thermal/electrical conductor, good corrosion resistance.
  • Weighs ~1/3 of steel. Very good ductility.
  • Applications: Packaging, consumer durables, utensils, electrical appliances, airplanes/jet engines (75-85% of components).

2.7.4 Copper

  • Non-ferrous. Excellent heat/electricity conductor, good corrosion resistance.
  • Soft, tough, easy to mould, workable hot or cold.
  • Pure form: thermal/electrical uses.
  • Alloys:
    • Brass (Cu + Zn): Utensils, household fittings, decorative objects.
    • Bronze (Cu + Sn): Good corrosion resistance. Used for valves, bearings.
    • Brass and bronze can be machined at high speeds for fine surface finish.

2.7.5 Lead

  • Durable, versatile. High density, easy workability. Good corrosion resistance (many acids don't affect it).
  • Used in soldering (lead-tin alloy).

2.7.6 Zinc

  • Low melting point. Fourth most used metal (after Fe, Al, Cu).
  • Applications (zinc alloys): Carburetors, fuel pumps, automobile parts.

2.7.7 Tin

  • Soft, ductile, very good malleability.
  • Resists corrosion in wet/dry conditions; used as protective coating for iron/steel.
  • Tin-base white metals for bearings (high pressure/load).
  • High malleability: tin cans for food.

2.8 Selection of Material

Crucial decision, considering physical and mechanical properties. Can dictate manufacturing process and affect final cost (material cost $\approx$ 50% of manufacturing cost).

Stepwise approach:

  1. Define material requirements: Based on overall objectives (cost, durability, life, function).
    • Example (Coins): Good ductility, hardness, corrosion resistance, high plasticity, lightweight.
  2. Find possible material(s): List materials meeting most requirements. (e.g., for coins: gold, silver, copper, alloys).
  3. Make a choice: Consider influencing factors/restrictions (availability, cost, aesthetics).
    • Example (Razor blades): Good malleability, corrosion resistance, abrasive resistance, economical. Edge may be alloyed for hardness/sharpness retention. Stainless steel is common.

2.9 Solved Examples

Example 2.1

A steel rod (10 mm diameter, 1.5 m length) pulled by 1 kN. E = $205 \times 10^9 \text{ N/m}^2$ (Note: PDF says $10^6 \text{ kN/m}^2$, which is $10^9 \text{ N/m}^2$).

  • Given: D = 10 mm = 0.01 m, L = 1.5 m, F = 1 kN = 1000 N, E = $205 \times 10^9 \text{ N/m}^2$.
  • Cross-sectional area A: $\pi D^2/4 = \pi(0.01)^2/4 = 7.85 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m}^2$.
  • (a) Stress ($\sigma$): $F/A = 1000 \text{ N} / (7.85 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m}^2) = 1.27 \times 10^7 \text{ N/m}^2$.
  • (b) Strain ($\epsilon$): $\sigma/E = (1.27 \times 10^7 \text{ N/m}^2) / (205 \times 10^9 \text{ N/m}^2) = 6.21 \times 10^{-5}$ (m/m is implied, dimensionless). (PDF uses $10^5$, likely a typo if E is in N/m$^2$ and F in N. If F is in kN and E in kN/m$^2$, then $1 \text{ kN} / (7.85 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m}^2) / (205 \times 10^6 \text{ kN/m}^2) = 6.21 \times 10^{-5}$. My calculation matches PDF answer with consistent units.)
  • (c) Elongation ($\Delta L$): $\epsilon \times L = (6.21 \times 10^{-5}) \times 1.5 \text{ m} = 9.32 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m}$.

Example 2.2

Tensile test on steel (D=30mm, L=200mm). Extension at 50kN load = 0.1mm. Diameter at failure = 20mm. Total extension = 50mm.

  • Given: $D_{\text{initial}}$ = 30 mm, $L_{\text{initial}}$ = 200 mm. At F = $50 \times 10^3$ N, $\Delta L$ = 0.1 mm.
  • Area (initial, $A_0$): $\pi(30)^2/4 = 706.86 \text{ mm}^2 = 706.86 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}^2$.
  • Stress (at 50kN): $(50 \times 10^3 \text{ N}) / (706.86 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}^2) = 7.07 \times 10^7 \text{ N/m}^2$.
  • Strain (at 0.1mm extension): $\Delta L/L = 0.1 \text{ mm} / 200 \text{ mm} = 0.0005$.
  • (a) Young's Modulus (E): $\text{Stress/Strain} = (7.07 \times 10^7 \text{ N/m}^2) / 0.0005 = 1.414 \times 10^{11} \text{ N/m}^2$ (or 141.4 GPa).
  • (b) Percentage elongation (at fracture): $(\text{Total extension / Original length}) \times 100 = (50 \text{ mm} / 200 \text{ mm}) \times 100 = 25\%$.
  • (c) Percentage decrease in area (at fracture):
    • Area_final ($A_f$): $\pi(20)^2/4 = 314.16 \text{ mm}^2$.
    • Decrease in area: $A_0 - A_f = 706.86 - 314.16 = 392.7 \text{ mm}^2$.
    • $((A_0 - A_f) / A_0) \times 100 = (392.7 / 706.86) \times 100 = 55.55\%$.
    • (Or using $(D^2 - d^2) / D^2 \times 100 = (30^2 - 20^2) / 30^2 \times 100 = (900 - 400) / 900 \times 100 = 500/900 \times 100 = 55.55\%$).

Example 2.3

Timber post, rectangular, one side twice the other. Compressive load 10kN, contracts 0.0521mm over 1m length. E_timber = 12 GPa. Find dimensions.

  • Given: F = $10 \times 10^3$ N, $\Delta L = 0.0521 \times 10^{-3}$ m, $L_0 = 1$ m, E = $12 \times 10^9 \text{ N/m}^2$.
  • Using $\Delta L = FL_0 / (AE)$ (Eq. 2.5).
  • $A = FL_0 / (\Delta L \times E) = (10 \times 10^3 \text{ N} \times 1 \text{ m}) / (0.0521 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m} \times 12 \times 10^9 \text{ N/m}^2) = 0.015994 \text{ m}^2 \approx 0.016 \text{ m}^2$.
  • Let sides be $b$ and $d$. $d = 2b$.
  • Area $A = b \times d = b \times 2b = 2b^2$.
  • $2b^2 = 0.016 \text{ m}^2$
  • $b^2 = 0.008 \text{ m}^2$
  • $b = \sqrt{0.008} \approx 0.0894 \text{ m}$ (or 89.4 mm).
  • $d = 2b \approx 2 \times 0.0894 \approx 0.1788 \text{ m}$ (or 178.8 mm).
