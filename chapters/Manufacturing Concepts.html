Chapter 1: Manufacturing Concepts

1.1 Introduction

  • Core Idea: Manufacturing is the process of transforming raw materials (materials as found in nature, like iron ore, wood, crude oil) into useful products (like pens, cars, furniture) that satisfy human needs.
  • This involves:
    1. Converting raw material into a usable form with desired properties.
    2. Shaping the material into parts.
    3. Assembling parts to create the final object (product).
  • The overall process is called the manufacturing process.

The 5 Ms of Manufacturing (Page 2)

  • Manufacturing is vital for any industrialized nation's economy.
  • To carry out manufacturing, you essentially need 5 key inputs, often called the 5 Ms:
    1. Material: The raw or processed substance the product is made from.
    2. Methods: The specific techniques and procedures used (like casting, machining).
    3. Machinery: The tools, machines, and equipment needed.
    4. Men (Manpower): The people who operate machines, apply methods, and manage the process.
    5. Money: The financial investment required for materials, machinery, manpower, etc.

1.2 Manufacturing Processes

There are many ways to make a product. These are broadly classified into five main groups:

1.2.1 Casting

  • What: Melting material (usually metal) and pouring it into a mould (a cavity with the desired shape). The material then solidifies, taking the shape of the mould.
  • Key Features: Good for intricate shapes, can make very large or very small products.
  • Example: Making engine blocks, frying pans, machine bases. (See Figure 1.2 for a visual).

1.2.2 Machining

  • What: Removing unwanted material from a workpiece using cutting tools on a machine tool (like a lathe or milling machine) to achieve the desired shape and size. When applied to metals, it's often called metal cutting.
  • Key Features: Can achieve high precision and tight tolerances (very accurate dimensions). Results in waste material (scrap).
  • Examples: Drilling holes, making gears, nuts, bolts, turning a shaft on a lathe. (See Figure 1.3 showing turning on a lathe).

1.2.3 Forming

  • What: Shaping material (usually metal) by applying forces (like compression, tension) that cause it to permanently deform (change shape) without removing material.
  • Key Features: No material is wasted as scrap; it's just reshaped.
  • Examples: Rolling metal sheets, forging parts, drawing wire, making kitchen utensils, car body panels. (See Figure 1.4 showing the rolling process).

1.2.4 Powder Metallurgy

  • What: Blending fine material powders, pressing them into a desired shape in a die, and then heating (sintering) them in a controlled atmosphere so the particles bond together.
  • Key Features: Good dimensional accuracy and finish, often requires no further processing. Can create parts from materials that are hard to machine.
  • Examples: Making certain types of gears, tungsten filaments for light bulbs, some cutting tools.

1.2.5 Joining

  • What: Assembling two or more separate pieces to form a single product. The individual pieces might have been made using any of the above processes.
  • Types:
    • Permanent: Welding, brazing, soldering (involve heating, often melting, and sometimes adding filler material).
    • Temporary/Semi-permanent: Using fasteners like nuts, bolts, screws, rivets, or adhesives.
  • Key Features: Essential for creating complex products made of multiple parts. Widely used in fabrication.
  • Examples: Welding parts of a ship's hull, bolting engine components together, soldering electronic components. (See Figure 1.5 showing different joint types).

1.3 Allied Activities

Besides the core shaping processes, manufacturing involves other essential activities:

1.3.1 Measurement

  • What: Checking the dimensions (size, shape, smoothness, etc.) of parts during and after manufacturing to ensure they meet the design specifications.
  • Why: To guarantee the product functions correctly and parts fit together. Includes inspection.
  • Example: Using calipers to check the diameter of a machined rod.

1.3.2 Assembly

  • What: The process of putting individual manufactured components together to create the final product. (Distinct from joining, which might be one step within making a component or during assembly).
  • Why: Most products (like pens, cars, computers) are made from multiple parts that need to be assembled.
  • Example: Putting together the body, refill, cap, etc., to make a pen; assembling the thousands of parts that make up a car. (See Figure 1.6 showing major car parts).

1.3.3 Property Changing

  • What: Processes specifically designed to alter the properties of a material (like hardness, strength) to make it suitable for its function or easier to manufacture.
  • Why: Sometimes a material needs to be soft for shaping but hard for final use. Material selection is crucial here.
  • Example: Heat treating steel parts (like gears or tools) to make them harder and more wear-resistant after they have been machined or formed.

1.4 Selection of Manufacturing Process

  • Often, a part could be made by more than one process. The choice depends on:
    1. Volume of Production: How many identical parts are needed? (influences cost per part).
    2. Expected Quality & Properties: What level of precision, surface finish, or material strength is required?
    3. Technical Viability: Is the process capable of making the required shape and features?
    4. Economy: What is the total cost (materials, labor, tooling, machine time)?

1.5 Types of Production

Manufacturing activities are often categorized based on the quantity and variety of products:

1.5.1 Job Shop Production

  • Characteristics: Low volume (often just one or a few), high variety, usually custom-made to specific customer orders. Uses general-purpose machines.
  • Examples: A custom-built racing car, a specific replacement part for old machinery, a prototype, a special tool.

1.5.2 Batch Production

  • Characteristics: Medium volume (products made in batches or lot sizes), moderate variety. The same equipment might be used for different batches of different products. Orders may be repetitive.
  • Examples: Batches of furniture, textbooks, clothing.

1.5.3 Mass Production

  • Characteristics: High volume, low (or no) variety (identical products). Uses specialized, dedicated machines and assembly lines for maximum efficiency and low cost per unit. High initial investment.
  • Examples: Pens, screws, standard nuts and bolts, cars on an assembly line, smartphones.

1.6 Professionalism and Ethics

  • Core Idea: Engineers and technologists involved in manufacturing have a responsibility to society. Products must be safe and perform reliably.
  • Ethics: Adherence to high ethical standards and codes (like the IEEE code mentioned) is expected to ensure public safety and trust.

1.7 Safety

  • Core Idea: Safety is paramount in manufacturing due to inherent hazards. It's everyone's responsibility ("Safety First").
  • Hazards: Manufacturing involves many potential dangers (See Table 1.1):
    • Moving/rotating machine parts
    • Hot materials (molten metal, hot chips)
    • Sharp tools and workpiece edges
    • Electrical shocks
    • Noise, dust, fumes
    • Falling objects
    • Spills (oil, grease)
  • Causes of Accidents: Often human error (negligence, lack of training, haste), but also unsafe equipment or conditions.
  • Ensuring Safety:
    • Equipment Design: Machines have guards, safety stops, sensors. These must never be bypassed.
    • Regulations (1.7.1): Governments enforce safety through laws (Factory Acts, Industrial Safety Acts) and standards. Employers have legal responsibilities.
    • General Safety Considerations (1.7.2 - Key Practices):
      1. Wear appropriate clothing (no loose items), proper shoes.
      2. Always wear safety glasses.
      3. Get proper training before using any machine; know how to stop it quickly.
      4. Use tools correctly and ensure they are in good condition.
      5. Clamp workpieces securely before machining/processing.
      6. Be aware of moving parts; don't reach near them.
      7. Handle freshly cut metal/chips carefully (sharp edges).
      8. Handle cutting tools carefully (very sharp).
      9. Keep guards in place; don't tamper with safety devices.
      10. Be cautious with electrical equipment, especially near water or if wires are damaged.
      11. Report all injuries, even minor ones, immediately.
      12. Avoid working when overly tired or distracted.
